Clinical Rationale

Why comorbidities make shingles vaccination urgent

The tool explains the bidirectional link between each patient's conditions and shingles in plain language — driving patient understanding and vaccination intent at the point of care.

Diabetes Mellitus
Diabetes weakens immune surveillance allowing dormant virus to reactivate
⇄
Shingles pain triggers cortisol that raises blood sugar and destabilises glucose control
Diabetics face twice the normal risk of shingles. A shingles episode then makes diabetes harder to manage — vaccination breaks this clinical cycle.
Cardiovascular Disease
Heart disease lowers immune resilience against viral reactivation
⇄
Shingles nearly doubles heart attack risk and raises stroke risk 35% in the year after infection
Shingles triggers vascular inflammation that can destabilise arterial plaques — a serious risk for patients already living with CVD.
Chronic Kidney Disease
Uraemic immunosuppression allows unchecked viral reactivation
⇄
Antiviral treatment for shingles is significantly complicated by impaired renal clearance
CKD patients face 3× higher shingles risk. When it occurs, treatment is harder — prevention is far superior to managing a shingles episode in a CKD patient.
Immunocompromised
Immunosuppression removes T-cell surveillance that keeps the virus dormant
⇄
Disseminated zoster can spread to internal organs and the brain — significant mortality
In immunocompromised patients, shingles can rapidly progress to disseminated disease. The recombinant vaccine is specifically approved for this group.
